    Purpose

    The purpose of our project is to explore the connections between the wealth and development of different countries in connection with the quality of their education. Through our project, we hope to bring attention toward the discrepancies in the quality of education on a global scope.

    Comparing Attendance, Completion, and Out-of-School Rates

    The data visualizations below are created using a combination of data from three separate UNICEF datasets: one on net attendance rates per country, one on school completion rates per country, and one on out-of-school rates per country. Each data-set is also split up by grade level (Primary, Lower Secondary, and Upper Secondary). For each grade level, the data sets compare the percentage rates of female and males in each country that attend school, complete school, or are out of school. They also compare the rates for school children within five separate wealth quintiles (categorized by UNICEF as Poorest, Second, Middle, Fourth, or Richest). The visualizations plot attendance rates and completion rates against each other, differentiating between gender or wealth level in order to compare and identify disparities. The color of the points identify which region the country is a part of and by hovering on each point, the country name is displayed. The visualizations also allow comparisons to be made between the rates for each grade level. Second, there are visualizations displaying out of school rates for each country on a map with the color displaying the percentage rate. The country name can be displayed by hovering over each country. These maps allow comparisons to be made between out of school rates for each gender, wealth level, and grade levels.

    Gender

    • EAP - East Asia and the Pacific
    • ECA - Europe and Central Asia
    • LAC - Latin America and the Caribbean
    • MENA - Middle East and North Africa
    • NA - North America
    • SA - South Asia
    • SSA - Sub-Saharan Africa

    • For both genders, there is a direct correlation seen between attendance rates and completion rates, demonstrating that the countries whose students attend school the most consistently have the highest completion rates.
    • There is a clear trend for both genders that the higher the grade level, the lower the attendance and completion rates.
    • While there isn’t a drastic disparity between male and female attendance and completion rates, there is still lower rates for females than males.
    • Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) by far has the lowest attendance and completion rates in each of its countries.
    • European and Central Asian (ECA) countries tend to have the consistently highest rates for all grade levels.

    • Out of school rates have an inverse relationship with the attendance and completion rates from above--as the grade level gets higher, out of school rates for both genders and in every region get higher.
    • Once again, while there isn’t a drastic disparity between female and male out of school rates, there still seems to be slightly higher rates for females than males.
    • African countries tend to have the highest out of school rates while Latin American and Central Asian countries tend to have the lowest.

    Foundational Learning Skills

    For this dataset, UNICEF calculated the percentage of children in two different age groups (children in Grades 2/3 and children aged 7 to 14) who demonstrated foundational reading skills and the percentage of children in the same two different age groups (children in Grades 2/3 and children aged 7 to 14) who demonstrated foundational numeracy skills. These values were collected from a set of countries in particular regions specified below. In the following visualizations, the percentage of children who demonstrated foundational reading skills is plotted against the percentage of children who demonstrated foundational numeracy skills for a given country. The plotted values are distinguished by color to signify the region the data is from. In the first visualization, the values are separated into two scatterplots to group the data by gender. In the second visualization, the values are separated into five scatterplots to group the data by wealth quintile.

    Gender

    • EAP - East Asia and the Pacific
    • ECA - Europe and Central Asia
    • LAC - Latin America and the Caribbean
    • MENA - Middle East and North Africa
    • NA - North America
    • SA - South Asia
    • SSA - Sub-Saharan Africa
    • Across all the data, there is not an extremely noticeable difference between the percentage of girls with demonstrated numeracy/reading skills and the percentage of boys with demonstrated numeracy/reading skills.
    • There is a positive trend between the percentage of children with numeracy skills and the percentage of children with reading skills. (If a country has a high percentage of children who have demonstrated reading skills, then the country likely has high percentage of children who have demonstrated numeracy skills.)
    • Children aged 7 to 14 have high higher percentages of children with demonstrated numeracy/reading skills than children in grade 2/3.
    • The SSA (Sub-Saharan) region has the lowest percentages of children with demonstrated numeracy/reading skills across gender, whereas the ECA (Europe and Central Asia) and EAP (East Asia and the Pacific) regions seem to have the highest percentages of children with demonstrated numeracy/reading skills.
    • The LAC (Latin America and the Caribbean) region has the least amount of data represented in this dataset.
